A Spatial Analysis of Changing Flight Corridors

The Legacy Network

September 2025 – January 8, 2026. The baseline regional structure (Brighter Blue) before the reorganization.

Transition

Observing the disappearance of traditional hubs as the operational focus shifts.

A Fundamental Shift

On January 9, 2026, the network was entirely reconfigured. Routes were no longer optimized for passenger demand, but for a specialized logistics chain (Orange).

New Operational Flow

January 9, 2026 – Present. The Orange corridors highlight current high-frequency routes connecting major processing zones.

The Shadow Map

By overlaying the flight data with ICE infrastructure, a pattern of coordination emerges.

Infrastructure Overlap

Nationwide detention facilities (red). The flight network now mirrors the density of the detention system.

Regional Focus: Minneapolis

The Twin Cities serve as a northern anchor, where flight arrivals frequently coincide with regional transfer activity.

Regional Focus: Gulf Coast

Louisiana remains a primary hub for this logistics chain, with multiple facilities positioned within short flight-radii.

Regional Focus: El Paso

The West Texas sector shows the most intense concentration of border infrastructure.

Return to the Origin

Back in the Midwest, corridors bypass traditional hubs in favor of specific Great Lakes facilities.

Analysis Conclusion

The transformation of Air Wisconsin’s network represents a broader shift in how regional aviation assets are utilized.

DATA SOURCES: Flight data via ADS-B Exchange (Sept 2025 – Feb 2026). Facility data via ICE Average Daily Population (ADP) reports. Map produced for Wisconsin Watch.
